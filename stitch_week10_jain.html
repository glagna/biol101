Key Concepts

Microglia: Resident immune cells in the brain. They play active roles in development and synaptic pruning.

Neurogenesis: Birth of new neurons. Important for learning and memory.

GABAergic neurons: Neurons using GABA as a neurotransmitter. Typically inhibitory.

IGF1: Signaling protein that promotes cell growth and survival.

Knockout model: Genetically engineered organism with a specific gene disabled.

The Power of Good Questions

Use the question taxonomy below to frame your thinking. All four types of questions are valuable and welcomed:

Clarification

"What does [term] mean?" or "Can you explain [concept] in simpler terms?"

Method

"Why did you use [approach] instead of [alternative]?" or "How did you control for [variable]?"

Interpretation

"Could there be an alternative explanation for [finding]?" or "What about [confounding factor]?"

Extension

"What would happen if [scenario]?" or "How would this approach work for [related disease]?"

Don't worry about asking a "perfect" question. Researchers appreciate genuine curiosity and thoughtful engagement.

Post-Seminar Reflection

By Monday, June 8, respond to both prompts below in approximately 250–350 words combined. This is at the highest Bloom's level: "Create/Synthesize."

Prompt 1: Propose a Follow-Up Experiment (100–150 words)

Based on Dr. Jain's talk, propose one follow-up experiment that could extend or test her findings. Structure your response around three components:

• The Biological Question

What specifically do you want to understand? Example: "I want to know whether microglia-derived IGF1 is necessary for GABAergic neurogenesis in disease contexts."

• Why This Matters

How does answering this question build on or challenge Jain's findings?

• What You'd Measure

What evidence would tell you the answer?

Prompt 2: Career Reflection (100–150 words)

Across the quarter, you've heard from six researchers at different career stages. Which path resonates most with where you see yourself in 10 years, and why? (It's okay if the answer is "none of the above." Explain what you'd do instead.)
