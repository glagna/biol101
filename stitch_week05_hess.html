school Background & Career

Dr. David Hess is a Genomic Scientist at the Nevada State Public Health Lab and faculty member at the University of Nevada, Reno (UNR) School of Medicine. He is the former Chair of the Biology Department at Santa Clara University, where he developed innovative approaches to infectious disease surveillance using cutting-edge genomic sequencing.

His work bridges fundamental molecular biology with real-world public health action. Dr. Hess's research focuses on using whole-genome sequencing to monitor pathogenic organisms, track outbreaks in real time, and inform public health interventions that protect communities from emerging threats.

Study Guide: Key Concepts

Candida auris: Emerging fungal pathogen first identified in 2009; spreads readily in healthcare settings and is often resistant to multiple antifungal drugs.

Whole-genome sequencing (WGS): A technique that determines the complete DNA sequence of an organism, enabling precise identification of genetic variants and relationships between isolates.

Single-nucleotide polymorphism (SNP): A variation at a single DNA base pair between individuals; used to measure how closely related different pathogen strains are.

Clade: A genetically distinct group of organisms sharing a common ancestor. C. auris has five known clades, each associated with a different geographic origin.

Phylogenetic analysis: A method for examining evolutionary relationships between organisms using genetic data, often visualized as a branching tree diagram.

Antifungal resistance: The ability of a fungus to survive exposure to drugs designed to kill or inhibit it, making infections harder to treat.

Genomic surveillance: The systematic use of DNA sequencing to monitor pathogen populations, track outbreaks, and guide public health decisions.

The Power of Good Questions

Use the question taxonomy below to frame your thinking. All four types of questions are valuable and welcomed:

Clarification

"What does [term] mean?" or "Can you explain [concept] in simpler terms?"

Method

"Why did you use [approach] instead of [alternative]?" or "How did you control for [variable]?"

Interpretation

"Could there be an alternative explanation for [finding]?" or "What about [confounding factor]?"

Extension

"What would happen if [scenario]?" or "How would this approach work for [related disease]?"

Don't worry about asking a "perfect" question. Researchers appreciate genuine curiosity and thoughtful engagement.

Choose one or two prompts that resonate with you. Your total reflection should be ~200–300 words — you don't need to answer every prompt.

A Note on Reflection

Reflections are not formal essays. Write conversationally, but thoughtfully. Be honest: if something confused you, say so. If the research didn't grab you, that's okay—explain what would have made it more engaging. The goal is to deepen your thinking, not to impress with jargon.
