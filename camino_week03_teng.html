Study Guide

Glossary: Key Concepts in Tissue Mechanics

Start here if you're new to thinking about cells as physical objects.

Cellular migration: The movement of individual cells through tissue, driven by cell-cell contacts and mechanical forces.
Tissue fusion: The process by which two separate tissues merge into one, requiring coordinated cell migration and shape changes.
Craniofacial development: The formation of the head, face, and jaw during embryonic development—a highly organized process requiring precise cell movements.
Mechanical forces: Physical stresses (tension, compression, shear) that act on cells and tissues and influence their behavior.
Traction forces: Forces that cells exert on their surroundings, measurable using specialized microscopy techniques.
Cleft lip/palate: A birth defect where tissues don't fuse properly during development, affecting about 1 in 600 births. Understanding normal fusion helps explain when it goes wrong.

As you read the paper, jot down any terms you encounter that aren't on this list. Bring them to class—asking "What does [term] mean?" is always a great question.

Meet the Speaker

Background & Training

Camilla is a postdoctoral researcher in the Bush Laboratory at UCSF's Department of Cell & Tissue Biology, where she studies how mechanical forces guide cell migration and tissue fusion during craniofacial development. She completed her PhD in developmental biology, focusing on the cellular and molecular mechanisms underlying face formation.

Her work bridges experimental embryology and mechanical biology: she combines live imaging, force measurement, and molecular analysis to understand how cells "know" when and where to fuse, with implications for understanding and potentially preventing birth defects.

The Power of Good Questions

This week, challenge yourself to ask Method or Interpretation questions—not just clarifications. Use the taxonomy below as a guide:

Clarification

"What does [term] mean?" or "Can you explain [concept] in simpler terms?"

Method

"Why did you use [approach] instead of [alternative]?" or "How did you measure [force/process]?"

Interpretation

"Could there be an alternative explanation for [finding]?" or "What about [confounding variable]?"

Extension

"What would happen if [scenario]?" or "Could this mechanism apply to [other tissue]?"

Don't worry about asking a "perfect" question. Researchers appreciate genuine curiosity and thoughtful engagement.

Post-Seminar Reflection

Reflection Prompts

Dr. Vacante (Week 2) used CRISPRa to study gene regulation in blood vessel cells. Dr. Teng studies mechanical forces in developing tissues. What do these two lines of research have in common, and how do they differ in their approach to understanding human disease?

2. Explaining a Technique

A Note on Reflection

Reflections are not formal essays. Write conversationally, but thoughtfully. Be honest about what you understood and what confused you. The goal is to deepen your thinking, not to impress with jargon.
