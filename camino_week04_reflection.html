Before Class: Metacognitive Reflection

Due by 11:59 PM Tuesday, April 21. Submit a brief written reflection (300–400 words) answering the three prompts below. Be honest and thoughtful—this is for you, and your reflections help shape how we discuss things in class.

Prompt 1
How has your understanding of scientific research changed since Week 1?
Think about how you thought about research on April 1 vs. now. Has anything shifted? Do you understand the work differently? Have you changed how you think about what scientists do day-to-day? Be specific—reference something from Francesca's or Camilla's talk.
Prompt 2
What was one moment in the first three weeks where you felt confused, surprised, or enlightened?
Pick a specific moment from one of the seminars or readings. What happened? Why did it stick with you? Was the confusion resolved, or are you still wondering? (Unresolved confusion is fine—actually, it's great. It means you're genuinely engaged.)
Prompt 3
What questions do you have about your own future in biology?
Don't worry about knowing the answers. We're in Week 4—you're just beginning to explore. What are you curious about? Worried about? What feels possible? What feels intimidating? Your honest answers help us design the second half of the course.
Reflection tip: Write conversationally. These prompts are designed to help you think, not to be graded on perfection. If you find yourself stuck on a prompt, skip it and come back. The goal is to put your honest thinking on paper.

During Class: Career Paths & Exploration

Week 4's class is structured differently than speaker weeks. No lecture. Instead: conversation, small group discussion, and career exploration. Here's what we'll do:

Part 1: Career Landscape Tour (5:25–5:40, 15 min)

We'll map out the career paths of our six speakers and guests who are coming throughout the quarter. Each represents a different stage, field, and approach.

  • Francesca Vacante (Week 2): Postdoc, early independence, CRISPRa & non-coding RNA therapeutics, Stanford
  • Camilla Teng (Week 3): Postdoc, 2024 finalist in postdoc competitions, cell & tissue biology, UCSF
  • David Hess (Week 5): Transitioned from faculty to public health genomics, leadership role, Nevada
  • Julie Frouard (Week 6): Postdoc-level scientist, virology & immunology, Gladstone & UCSF
  • Emma Krasovich Southworth (Week 8): PhD candidate, interdisciplinary (ecology + epidemiology + climate), Stanford
  • Samhita Jain (Week 9): Clinician-scientist, neonatology + neuroscience, bridging medicine and bench research

What do you notice? Common themes? Different pathways? Places where you see yourself?

The Speakers' Career Paths

Academic Research

PhD → Postdoc → Faculty or Research Scientist role. Focus on discovery science and independent research programs.

Examples: Francesca, Camilla, Emma

Public Health & Policy

Use research skills to address population-level health challenges. Often work in government labs or public agencies.

Example: David Hess

Clinician-Scientist

Combine clinical practice (medicine, pediatrics) with bench research. Unique perspective bridging patient care and discovery.

Example: Samhita Jain

Biotechnology Industry

Work in biotech companies on drug development, diagnostics, or therapeutics. Combine science with product development.

Possible path for many fields

Science Communication

Write, teach, or explain science to public audiences. Could be science journalism, education, or museum work.

Alternative path building on research skills

Other Paths

Law (biotech IP), business, consulting, management. Biology training opens many doors beyond the lab.

Diverse possibilities with transferable skills

Part 2: Small Group Discussion (5:40–6:10, 30 min)

You'll split into groups of 3–4 and discuss one of these prompts (assigned by Giorgio):

  • Career Goals: "If you imagine yourself in 5 years, what does your ideal scientific life look like? Where do you work? What do you spend your time on? Why?"
  • Pivots & Redirects: "Career paths aren't always linear. Have any of the speakers made unexpected turns? What appeals to you about non-traditional paths?"
  • Values & Fulfillment: "What matters most to you in a career—discovery, helping people, creative problem-solving, work-life balance, something else? How does that map to different paths?"
  • Skills Transfer: "If you left research, what skills would you take with you? How are biology research skills valuable in unexpected fields?"

One person will take notes to share with the class.

Part 3: Sharing & Synthesis (6:10–6:25, 15 min)

Each group shares a 1–2 minute summary of their discussion. As you listen to other groups, look for patterns and differences in how people think about their futures. There's no "right" answer—just different paths and values.

Part 4: Informational Interview Workshop (6:25–6:30, 5 min)

Quick overview of the informational interview assignment due Week 7:

  • Identify someone in a field/role you're curious about
  • Email them with a brief, specific request (15–20 min conversation)
  • Come prepared with 5–7 thoughtful questions
  • In Week 7, you'll share 3–5 min summary with the class

We'll provide a list of contacts, a template email, and suggested questions. More details below.

Looking ahead: Start planning your informational interview now — it's due in Week 7 (May 13). Think about someone in a role or field that interests you. This could be a connection you already have, someone a professor can introduce you to, or a professional you find through LinkedIn. The sooner you reach out, the more likely they'll have time to meet with you.

Some Final Thoughts

By Week 4, you've watched two researchers present their work and thought deeply about what it means to read and understand science. You might be feeling:

  • Excited: "I love this! I could see myself doing this work!"
  • Overwhelmed: "I don't understand anything. Am I cut out for science?"
  • Uncertain: "This is interesting, but I'm not sure it's for me."
  • Curious: "I want to know more about different paths."

All of these are normal and valuable. The next two weeks will give you more perspectives. By June, you'll have a much clearer sense of what resonates with you and what doesn't. That clarity is the whole point of this course.
