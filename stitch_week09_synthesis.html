Before Class: Identify One Connecting Theme

Due by 11:59 PM Monday, May 25. Review your five post-class reflections from Weeks 2, 3, 5, 6, and 8. Write a short paragraph (~100 words):

assignment

Look Across All Five Talks

Identify one theme or question that connects at least three of the five speakers. Write a short paragraph explaining the connection. What is the theme? How does it show up in each of the talks? Why does this connection matter?

Possible themes to explore:

  • • Methods: Did multiple speakers use sequencing, CRISPR, modeling, or other shared techniques?
  • • Career paths: Notice how several speakers are postdocs, or how some made unexpected transitions?
  • • The gap between basic research and human health: How do different researchers bridge bench and bedside?
  • • Interdisciplinarity: How do speakers combine different fields?
  • • How researchers frame uncertainty: How comfortable are they with not knowing?
lightbulb

Reflection tip: Write conversationally. The goal is to notice connections and articulate them for yourself—and for your classmates when you share today.

During Class: Concept Mapping & Synthesis

Week 9 is a working class. No lecture, no speaker. Just you, your classmates, and the ideas you've been gathering all quarter.

map

Part 1: Concept Mapping (25 minutes)

You'll work in groups of 3–4 to create a visual "concept map" on a large sheet of paper. At the center, write "Biology Research in 2026." Around it, add the five speakers (Francesca, Camilla, David, Julie, Emma) with their topics, methods, career stages, and anything else important. Then draw connections: shared methods, shared motivations, shared career features, shared skills. Each group presents their map (2 minutes each).

forum

"What I Wish I'd Known" Discussion (15 minutes)

Whole class reflection:

  • • What do you know now about scientific research that you didn't know in Week 1? Be specific.
  • • What surprised you most across the talks? What stuck with you?
  • • What kinds of skills do these researchers share, beyond their specific scientific expertise? Think about communication, persistence, collaboration, comfort with uncertainty, resilience.
preview

Part 3: Final Synthesis Preview (15 minutes)

Introducing the Final Synthesis Reflection: Due Week 11. Approximately 400 words. Three components:

  • • Part 1: Connections. Choose a theme, method, or question from at least three talks. Explain how it manifested in each.
  • • Part 2: Growth. How has your approach to reading scientific papers changed since Week 1?
  • • Part 3: Looking Forward. Based on everything you experienced this quarter, what is one thing you want to explore, learn, or do next?

Concept Mapping Guide

Need help visualizing the connections? Here's a structure to get your group started:

How to Build Your Concept Map

Start with the center

Write "Biology Research in 2026" in the middle of your paper. Everything else radiates from this central idea.

Add the five speakers

Around the center, add nodes for each speaker. Include their names and a one-sentence description of their work: Francesca Vacante (CRISPRa, non-coding RNA, postdoc), Camilla Teng (tissue fusion, craniofacial biology, postdoc), David Hess (genomic surveillance, public health), Julie Frouard (HIV sequencing, virology), Emma Krasovich Southworth (tropical cyclones & dengue, PhD candidate).

Add their methods

For each speaker, jot down key techniques: CRISPR, sequencing, modeling, microscopy, fieldwork, computational analysis.

Draw connections

Draw lines between speakers where connections exist. Label those lines. Examples: "Both use sequencing," "Both are postdocs," "Both bridge basic science and health outcomes," "Both use computational tools."

Step back and look

What patterns emerge? Are there clusters of speakers? Universal themes? A unifying question threading through all five talks? Your group's insights matter.
