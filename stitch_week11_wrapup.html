01 · Final Synthesis Reflection

Graded • 10 pts (P/NP) • Due Monday, June 8, 11:59 PM

The capstone for the course. About 400 words, three parts. A personal, reflective essay, not a research paper. We want your honest thinking, your real curiosity, your real growth.

Part 1: Connections (100–150 words)

Choose a theme, method, or question that appeared in at least three of the six talks. Explain how it manifested in each, and what this tells you about biology as a field.

Part 2: Growth (100–150 words)

How has your approach to reading scientific papers changed since Week 1? Be specific about what you do differently now and what still feels uncertain.

Part 3: Looking Forward (100–150 words)

Based on everything this quarter, name one thing you want to explore, learn, or do next. The most useful answers are small and specific.

Submit online as a PDF or .docx to the Camino assignment Week 11: Final Synthesis Reflection. There is no June 10 class meeting, so everything is turned in through Camino.

This is our gallery walk, the part where we get to see each other off. After your reflection is in, head to the Week 11 Peer Gallery discussion and post a quick public version of your reflection:

1. Your most surprising connection

One sentence on the connection across talks that surprised you most.

2. Your "Looking Forward" move

A sentence or two on the next step you named in Part 3 of your reflection.

3. Optional: a thank-you or a piece of advice

A shout-out to a talk, a classmate, or a moment that stuck. You can frame it as advice to a future BIOL 101 student.

Then reply to two classmates. Make it real: a resource, an encouragement, a "we should both reach out to that speaker," a connection you noticed too.

Reflecting on the Journey

You Have Accomplished

You listened to six scientists: Francesca Vacante (CRISPRa & non-coding RNA), Camilla Teng (tissue fusion in development), David Hess (genomic surveillance & public health), Julie Frouard (HIV virology), Emma Krasovich Southworth (climate & dengue ecology), and Samhita Jain (clinician-scientist, microglia & neurogenesis).

You conducted an informational interview with a real professional, or made a genuine attempt and learned from it, and shared what you found with your classmates.

You read six scientific papers and learned to ask questions about methods, interpretations, and extensions.

You reflected on your own thinking across the quarter and articulated how you have grown.

You showed up, in the discussion threads, in the concept maps, in the room every Wednesday evening. That is not nothing. That is everything.

Biology is a conversation, between past and present scientists, between theory and evidence, between what we know and what we still need to discover. For eleven weeks you have been part of that conversation. You have listened, asked questions, thought deeply, and connected ideas across very different fields.

Whatever comes next, research, medicine, public health, science communication, or something else entirely, you will carry these weeks with you. Reach out to someone you met. Take another biology class. Read more papers. Keep asking the Method and Interpretation questions. The world needs your curiosity and your willingness to ask hard questions.
