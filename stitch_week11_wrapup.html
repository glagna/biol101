Before Class: Final Synthesis Reflection

Due by Monday, June 8 at 11:59 PM, or submit in class on Wednesday, June 10. This is the capstone assignment for the course. Write approximately 400 words responding to the three prompts below.

assignment_turned_in

Final Synthesis Reflection

Part 1: Connections (100–150 words)

Choose a theme, method, or question that appeared in at least three of the six talks. Explain how it manifested in each, and what this tells you about biology as a field.

Examples: the ubiquity of sequencing across different fields; the gap between basic research and clinical impact; the role of computational tools in modern biology; how researchers frame uncertainty; the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration; how career paths are not always linear.

Part 2: Growth (100–150 words)

How has your approach to reading scientific papers changed since Week 1? Be specific: what do you do differently now when you encounter a new paper? What strategies have you developed? What are you more confident about, and what still feels uncertain?

Part 3: Looking Forward (100–150 words)

Based on everything you experienced this quarter—the talks, the informational interview, the class discussions, your reading—what is one thing you want to explore, learn, or do next in your biology education or career?

This could be a specific class, a research opportunity, a person you want to talk to, a question you want to keep thinking about, or a skill you want to develop.

Important note: This is not a research paper. It is a personal, reflective essay that connects what you have learned across the quarter. We want to hear your honest thinking, your genuine curiosity, your real growth—not a polished performance.

During Class: Gallery Walk, Fishbowl & Celebration

This is not a typical class. This is a celebration of what you've accomplished this quarter. Here's how we'll spend our final time together:

gallery_thumbnail Part 1: Gallery Walk of Concept Maps (10 minutes)

Remember the concept maps your groups created in Week 9? We've photographed them. Take 5–7 minutes to walk around and look at the different maps. Notice the different connections different groups made. Add sticky notes with new thoughts or questions. See how your thinking has evolved since Week 9.

groups Part 2: Fishbowl Discussion (20 minutes)

4–5 volunteers sit in a circle at the front. The rest of the class listens. The inner circle discusses:

"What is one thing you will remember from this course a year from now?"

After 10 minutes, we rotate in new voices. The beauty of this format: you don't feel pressure to respond immediately. You can listen, let things settle, and then speak if the moment feels right. Everyone's voice matters, but not everyone needs to speak.

mail Part 3: Letter to a Future Student (15 minutes)

Each of you writes a short letter (5–8 sentences) to a future BIOL 101 student who will take this course next year. What advice would you give them about how to get the most out of this course? What helped you? What surprised you? What would you do differently? What should future students know before they walk in on the first day?

These letters become a resource for next year's cohort. You are creating something that will help others.

celebration Part 4: Closing & Course Evaluations (15 minutes)

If you'd like, we'll read a few of your letters aloud (volunteers only). Then we'll take a moment to reflect on the quarter together. I'll share some of what I've learned from you. You'll fill out course evaluations. And we'll close.

This has been an honor. Thank you for showing up, asking questions, engaging honestly, and letting yourselves be changed by this experience.

For eleven weeks, you've been part of that conversation. You've listened. You've asked questions. You've thought deeply. You've connected ideas.

Whatever comes next—whether you pursue research, medicine, public health, science communication, or something else entirely—you will carry these eleven weeks with you. What Comes After Week 11

This course ends on June 10, but your learning continues. You now know people—the speakers, your classmates, maybe a professional from your informational interview. You now have questions—about papers, about careers, about what you want to do next. You now have skills—note-taking, question-asking, synthesis, reflection.

Use them. Reach out to someone you met. Take another biology class. Do research. Read more papers. Have another informational interview. Ask your professors about their work. And keep thinking about what it means to do science, to ask questions, and to contribute to human knowledge and health.

The world needs your curiosity, your care, and your willingness to ask hard questions. Thank you for spending this quarter with us. We're proud of you.
