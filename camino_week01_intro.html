Before Class

1
Read & Skim Two Papers

Primary: Read David Raff's "How to Read and Understand a Scientific Paper: A Guide for Non-Scientists" (2016). This short, practical guide will be your roadmap for the rest of the quarter. It's not a difficult read—maybe 20-30 minutes.

Secondary: Skim the Francesca Vacante et al. paper (CARMN/miR-143/145 CRISPRa, ATVB 2025). Don't try to understand every detail. Instead, follow Raff's method:

  • Read the abstract. What is the main question?
  • Look at the figures and their legends. What story do they tell?
  • Skim the introduction. Why does this problem matter?
  • Glance at the methods. What did they actually do?

You're not expected to understand all the science yet. Just get oriented. You'll deepen your knowledge during the seminar itself.

2
Post a Practice Question to Discussion Board

After reading Raff and skimming the Vacante paper, post one genuine question to the Week 1 Discussion Board by 5 PM (before class starts). This is practice—it won't be graded, just reviewed.

Your question could be about the science, the methods, the motivation, or even a term you don't recognize. The only rule: no yes/no questions.

This accomplishes two things: (1) it gets you thinking before class, and (2) during class, we'll discuss what makes a good question and use your posts as live examples.

How to Read a Paper: Live Walkthrough (20 minutes)

Using the Vacante paper as a live example, we'll:

  • Read the abstract together and identify the central question: What is CARMN? Why target it?
  • Study key figures and what they show about the cis-ON system and its effect on gene expression
  • Discuss the experimental design: controls, cell types, readouts
  • Point out the main findings and implications
  • Highlight what you DON'T need to understand yet (you will by next week)

The goal is not to teach you the entire paper, but to show you a method so you can tackle future papers independently.

3
The Art of Asking Questions (15 minutes)

We'll examine the Discussion Board posts you submitted. For each, we'll discuss: What makes the question productive? What would make it even better? What type of question is it?

Then, we'll introduce a simple taxonomy of scientific questions:

The Four Types of Scientific Questions

Clarification

You don't understand a term, concept, or result.

"What does 'cis-regulatory element' mean?"
"Why is CARMN important?"
Method

You're curious about how or why they did something.

"Why use HEK293T cells instead of cardiomyocytes?"
"How did they measure CARMN expression?"
Interpretation

You see an alternative explanation or a confound.

"Could the effect be due to [alternative]?"
"What about [control condition]?"
Extension

You want to push the idea further or apply it elsewhere.

"What would happen if...?"
"How would this work in disease models?"

The key point: All four types are valuable. Scientists ask all of them. Early in your career, Clarification questions are especially useful—they show you're engaged and often lead to good discussions.

4
Informational Interview Assignment (10 minutes)

Intro to the Week 7 assignment: You will conduct a 15–20 minute conversation with a scientist, clinician, or biology professional outside SCU. The goal is to learn about their career path, daily work, advice for undergraduates, and what they find fulfilling.

Who to interview: Anyone working in biology, medicine, or a field you're interested in. Could be a grad student at UCSF, a postdoc at Stanford, a biotech industry scientist, a physician, a science writer, etc.

How to reach out: We'll provide a list of suggested contacts and a template email. Key tips:

  • Be specific about what you want (15-min conversation, not a long commitment)
  • Explain why you're interested in their work
  • Be flexible with timing
  • Thank them afterward

You'll share a 3–5 minute summary with the class in Week 7.

Key Takeaways

You don't need to be an expert to ask good questions.

Confusion is normal and valuable. Scientists spend their careers not understanding things—then figuring them out. If something confuses you, that's information worth exploring.

Reading a paper is a skill you'll develop over time.

You won't understand every section the first time—and that's okay. Raff's method gives you a framework. By Week 5, you'll find it easier. By the end of the quarter, you'll be surprised at how much you can extract from a research paper.

This course is here to help you explore, not judge.

P/NP grading means we care about engagement, not perfection. If you're struggling, confused, or not interested in a particular topic, come talk to Giorgio. That's what office hours are for.

A Note Before You Leave

You're about to spend a quarter engaging with cutting-edge research and real scientists. That's exciting. Some of you will fall in love with a particular topic. Some of you will discover that research isn't for you—and that's valuable information too. Either way, you'll develop skills (reading papers, asking questions, thinking critically) that will serve you in any career.
